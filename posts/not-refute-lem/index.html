Non refutation of the law of excluded middle in intuitionistic logic

Sep 17, 2021 00:00 · 668 words · 4 minute read

Intuitionistic logic refers to a system of symbolic logic that differ from classical logic by removing the law of excluded middle (that is \(p \vee \neg p\)) and the double negation elimination (that is \(\neg \neg p \rightarrow p\)).

One of the central theorem that can be proved in intuitionistic logic, that will be our best friend in this post, is the non refutation of the law of excluded middle, which looks something like this:

\[ \neg \neg (A \vee \neg A) \]

Intuitionism focuses of positive construction of proofs, so the theorem shall be read as "Intuitionistic logic does not positively refute the law of excluded middle".

Remembering that \(\neg A\) is defined as \(A \rightarrow \bot\) the theorem is expressed equivalently like

\[ ((A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)) \rightarrow \bot) \rightarrow \bot \]

In this post we will prove it with two different methods: using natural deduction and using lambda calculus.

Proof using natural deduction

I assume some familiarity with the natural deduction proof style. You can read about it (more than enough to understand this derivation) on Wikipedia.

For typographical purposes I will split up the proof in two parts.

First let's prove that under the assumption \(w\) that \((A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)) \rightarrow \bot\) the formula \(A\rightarrow \bot\) holds.

\[ \dfrac{\dfrac{\dfrac{\dfrac{}{A \: \text{true}}u}{A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)\: \text{true}} \vee _I \qquad \dfrac{}{(A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)) \rightarrow \bot \: \text{true}}w}{\bot \: \text{true}} \rightarrow _E}{A \rightarrow \bot \: \text{true}}\rightarrow _I ^u \]

In this proof we have discharged the hypothesis \(u\) but not \(w\). I will call this proof \(P^w\) to indicate that there is the non-discharged assumption \(w\).

Now let's complete the proof.

\[ \dfrac{\dfrac{\dfrac{\dfrac{P^w}{A \rightarrow \bot \: \text{true}}}{A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)\: \text{true}} \vee _I \qquad \dfrac{}{(A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)) \rightarrow \bot \: \text{true}}w}{\bot \: \text{true}} \rightarrow _E}{((A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)) \rightarrow \bot)\rightarrow \bot\: \text{true}} \rightarrow _I ^w \]

And with that we have proven the theorem!

Notice that we have actually used the assumption \(w\) two times. This might seem strange at first, but it is allowed because the two assuptions are taken in the same subproof "above" the discharge.

Proof using lambda calculus

By the virtue of the Curry-Howard correspondence, we can try to find a lambda tem that has type

\[ ((A \vee (A \rightarrow \bot)) \rightarrow \bot) \rightarrow \bot \]

and we would have our proof. Lambda terms like this are sometimes called proof terms. It turns out that the term

\[ \lambda f. \: f \: (\text{right}(\lambda a. \: f \: \text{left}(a)))) \]

has the desired type. We could check this by hand, but that would require a lot of work. What we can instead do is try to import it into Haskell and let the typechecker do the hard work.

import Data.Void

thm :: ((Either a (a -> Void)) -> Void) -> Void
thm f = f (Right (\a -> f (Left a)))

or, more elegantly

thm f = f (Right (f . Left))

This indeed typechecks, so that is also a proof of our theorem!

Consequences

Even though the law of excluded middle does not hold in intuitionistic logic, it holds in this "double negated form". Now recall that, while in intuitionistic logic it is not valid that \(\neg \neg p \rightarrow p\), a very similar theorem holds:

\[ p \rightarrow \neg \neg p \]

Intuitively this means that everithing that is provable in intuitionistic logic is then provable in this double negated form, so in some sense all the theorems that are of the form \(\neg \neg p\) are all the theorems \(p\) that hold in intuitionistic logic, plus the law of excluded middle. The double negated form is then stronger than the "normal" form.

This central idea is formalized in the double negation translation, in particular in the Glivenko's theorem, that states that

If \(p\) is a propositional formula, \(p\) is a classical tautology iff \(\neg \neg p\) is an intuitionistic tautology

This results has been further extended to first-order logic.
